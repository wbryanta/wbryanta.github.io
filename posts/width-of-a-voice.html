I scored the internet's favorite “AI tells” against 15 great novelists. They flag the novelists.

Twelve folk tells, operationalized and scored: a coin flip that burns exactly the writers with the strongest voices — and what the statistical detectors actually measure.

Will Bryant · July 5, 2026 · stylometry, measurement

There’s a checklist going around for spotting AI-written prose: the em dash, “not X, but Y,” the rule-of-three, “delve,” corporate jargon, hedging. People lose jobs and grades over these tells. So I did the boring thing nobody does with folk wisdom: I operationalized all twelve as conservative regexes and counted them — in 390 windows of prose by fifteen celebrated novelists (Morrison, Ishiguro, Pynchon, McCarthy, Didion — 78 books, 3,500 words per window), and in 400 long-form fiction samples from eight current AI models, generated with no style instruction at all.

If the tells worked, the AI samples would light up and the novelists wouldn’t.

Combined — the way the checklists themselves combine them, unweighted — the twelve tells are a coin flip: AUC 0.506. Tune a threshold to catch half the machine samples and it falsely flags 50.8% of the novelist windows — including Ishiguro 24 of 30, Pynchon 16 of 20, and Morrison 34 of 45.

Some individual results, because the details are where the fun is:

  • The em dash — the internet’s favorite tell — is the best one, and it’s still weak. AUC 0.680, with a cluster-bootstrap CI so wide [0.48, 0.85] it kisses chance. Yes, current models over-use the em dash (median 3.7 per 1,000 words vs 1.7 for the novelists). No, you cannot accuse a specific writer with that.
  • Four tells run backwards. Humans — celebrated, canonical humans — use MORE exclamation marks (AUC 0.238), more superlatives (0.305), and more hedging (0.410) than the machines do. If you’re flagging prose for exclamation points, you’re flagging humans.
  • “Delve,” the poster-child tell, is degenerate on fiction. Median zero on both sides. Novelists don’t “delve,” and neither do models writing fiction.

There’s a deeper problem with the checklist than its accuracy: what it burns. The em dash is not an AI artifact — it’s a core tool of punctuated literary prose, and the models learned it from us. “Not X, but Y” is antithesis, a rhetorical figure writers have used for a couple of millennia; the rule of three is older than the codex. When a checklist teaches people to read ancient craft as machine residue, the practical result isn’t AI detection — it’s writers sanding the distinctive features out of their own prose to avoid accusation. The tells aren’t fingerprints of machines. Mostly they’re fingerprints of edited prose, and the false positives land exactly on the writers with strong, unusual voices.

What about the statistical detectors — the GPTZero-class tools that score the “vibe” of a whole text? Honest answer: on this task, they work. We ran a state-of-the-art probability-curvature detector over the same corpora and it separated the novelists from pure model output cleanly — no checklist required. But decomposing what it measures changes how much weight the score can carry. In our data, the large majority of the detector’s signal reduces to a single scalar: how statistically predictable the prose is to a language model — the low-entropy register that current post-training produces. That’s a detector of a register, not of origin. And the register is a moving target: it moves with vendor, settings, and version (turning up some models’ reasoning effort measurably shifts text along that axis toward the human band), so a tool calibrated on this year’s checkpoints quietly mismeasures next year’s. None of that makes the tools useless — it makes them screening instruments being used as verdicts. In settings where most of what you screen is human-written, even a good detector applied to an individual piece of writing produces mostly false accusations, and the tools don’t ship with the calibrated uncertainty that would let a teacher or editor know that.

Both failure directions matter. Without honest context on limitations, people keep getting falsely accused — and the accused are disproportionately distinctive stylists and non-native speakers, whose prose sits exactly where the scores misread. Meanwhile the reverse traffic flows freely: lightly-edited machine text passes without much trouble, which is only a problem when it’s fraudulent or undisclosed — and that is a norms and transparency problem, not one a scoring tool can solve. The measurement layer cannot carry the enforcement weight being piled on it. Measurement can tell you where a text sits; only disclosure can tell you whether that’s honest.

Nobody’s copy, but somebody anyway

The tells study fell out of a larger question: not “can you detect AI text?” but “how close can a model actually get to a specific writer?” — measured the way stylometry measures humans, against the author’s own range of variation. You calibrate a measurement space on published novelists, build each author an envelope from how much she differs from herself at a fixed length, and then ask whether an imitation lands inside it.

Two results from that work reframed how I think about “AI voice.”

First: asked to imitate a named author, models move the surface — lexical variety, repetition texture, tense and sentiment — but the layer stylometry actually uses to attribute authorship, the frequencies of little function words like of, but, the, barely moves: in our floor-compliant runs, prompting closes about 6% of the function-word gap, and none of the broader most-frequent-word gap (that movement is indistinguishable from zero). Naming the author raises envelope entry from 10.7% to 30.5% on the function-word layer — real, but most imitations never get inside — and handing the model the author’s actual opening pages beats telling it the author’s name for every informative model we could compare. The name is mostly a vibe; the text is data.

Second, and stranger: when the models write with no style instruction at all, their output typically lands far from every particular author — at full vocabulary, the median unprompted sample sits outside every one of the fifteen envelopes — though the widest envelopes (Murakami, Foster Wallace) admit plenty of generic AI prose, which is why every imitation number has to be read against that base rate. But run the attribution machinery on the models themselves and each one self-attributes at 97.8%, against a 12.5% chance rate. And here’s the twist: that’s not because the models are narrow — at matched length, a model’s variation from itself is about as wide as a novelist’s variation from herself. Each model is a stable, identifiable voice with real range. That’s the honest shape of the “AI voice” question: not a formless average, but a set of new voices that are easy to tell from each other and consistently far from any particular human they’re asked to become.

The part I care most about: killing my own results

One more disclosure, because it’s load-bearing for whether you should trust any of the numbers above. Over this project I commissioned independent adversarial reviews of my own headline results — three full cycles — with instructions to refute, not confirm. They caught real problems. Across those cycles, four of my own headline claims were retracted before anyone outside the project saw them. The most recent was the kind of result you dream about: a clean, significant finding (p = 0.011) about reasoning models and authorial voice. The review found two construct-validity failures the significance tests couldn’t see; the follow-up controls confirmed them; the claims are gone. The full adversarial record — the attacks, the retractions, what survived — ships with the paper’s companion repository, on purpose.

I mention this not as virtue signaling but as method: in a field this easy to fool yourself in — where a plausible number about “AI writing” will be quoted a thousand times before anyone checks it — the red team is not optional infrastructure. The tells study above survived it. That’s the only reason I’m comfortable asking you to repeat it.

This is a side artifact of a larger measurement study (The Width of a Voice: placing machine imitation inside authors’ own measured variation — preprint forthcoming). The short version of that one: when you ask a model to imitate a specific author, the surface texture moves, but the function-word layer that actually carries authorship barely does. The tells study is the flip side: the surface features people think give machines away mostly don’t.

If you check the data and find something wrong, I want to know — the repo issues are open, and “someone re-ran my numbers and broke them” would be the most useful outcome I could ask for.
