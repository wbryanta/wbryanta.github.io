Two new research programs, built to tell me no.

Calliope asks whether any open-weight model can actually carry long-form literary fiction. Polyhymnia asks whether a century of writing craft can be treated as evidence instead of vibes. Both are designed so that “no” is a publishable answer.

Will Bryant · July 21, 2026 · evaluation, long-form writing

The Width of a Voice measured one thing: when a model imitates an author, how far inside that author’s own variation does it actually land? That work taught me that the interesting questions about machine writing are measurement questions, and that the failure mode isn’t getting a wrong answer — it’s building an instrument that can’t tell you you’re wrong. So the next two programs are built instrument-first. They have names, because they’re going to be around a while.

Calliope

Calliope is the generation side: can any current open-weight model serve as a foundation for long-form literary fiction — not a paragraph of pastiche, but thousands of words of prose a serious reader wouldn’t put down? Long-form literary quality is a genuinely hostile measurement target. There’s no benchmark, the ground truth is contested, and every incentive points toward fooling yourself. So the project runs like an assay, not a demo: pre-registered protocols with thresholds frozen before any output exists, positive and negative controls, instrument validation on known-clean and known-dirty fixtures before a dollar of compute is spent, blinded human reading, and hard budget kill-rules enforced by fail-closed automation rather than good intentions.

So far the program has produced adversarial peer reviews, incident postmortems, and a considerably more hardened remote-execution control plane than I expected to need — because the operational failures taught as much as the models did. I have paid real (small, capped, pre-authorized) money to learn exactly how a healthy GPU server can sit there billing while doing no science, and the automation that now prevents it is honestly some of my favorite work in the project. Results will come with receipts attached. Until then, the discipline is the deliverable.

Polyhymnia

Polyhymnia is the knowledge side. A century of serious writing about the craft of fiction exists — Lubbock, Booth, and everything since — and most of it disagrees with itself in ways that are the point, not a bug to be averaged away. Polyhymnia treats that body of thought the way a careful researcher treats any evidence base: exact editions, honest provenance, dimensional rights controls (owning a book is not permission to train on it), and a typed data model that preserves who claimed what, on what authority, and who disagreed — instead of flattening it all into the generic writing advice every chatbot already recites.

It’s also an exercise in a problem that matters well beyond fiction: how to ground model behavior in contested human knowledge without laundering authority or erasing disagreement. The current phase is foundations — sourcing, schemas, fail-closed validation, adversarially reviewed protocols — and the effectiveness questions are deliberately still open. Whether any of this measurably improves the diagnosis or revision of a manuscript is an empirical question, and I intend to answer it the slow way, with experiments where a null result would be reported just as loudly.

What I’m not saying yet

Deliberately absent from this post: model names and verdicts, metrics, thresholds, assay designs, and every specific instrument. Those live in the lab notebooks until there are results worth attaching them to — partly because that’s how pre-registration is supposed to work, and partly because I’ve learned that the fastest way to ruin a measurement program is to start defending its early opinions in public. When something survives its own controls, you’ll read about it here, with the data.
